## 1. Context & purpose

### What we are building

We are building the Products feature for the catalog module — a complete system for creating, editing, and managing products with their variants (SKUs), flexible attributes, category assignments, and pricing. This is the core of the e-commerce platform: without products, there is nothing to sell. The feature gives catalog managers full control over the product lifecycle from initial draft through enrichment and review to publication on the storefront.

### Problem statement

Today the platform has database tables for products and SKUs, but no business logic governs how they behave. There is no way to create or manage products through the API. Product attributes are stored as unstructured data, which makes it impossible to filter, validate, or ensure data quality. There are no rules preventing incomplete or inconsistent products from appearing on the storefront. The catalog management team needs a structured, reliable system to manage the full product lifecycle.

### Who this is for

- **The catalog manager (admin)** — creates and enriches products, assigns attributes, sets prices on variants, and moves products through the review-and-publish workflow.
- **The customer** — browses and searches for products on the storefront, views product details with accurate attributes and pricing, and selects specific variants (size, color) to purchase.
- **The supplier** — owns the products they provide; referenced on each product record so the business can track which supplier provides which goods.

## 2. Scope

### What is included (MVP)

**P0 — Must ship:**

- **Product and variant management** — create, read, update, and soft-delete products with their variants (SKUs) as a single managed unit. A product owns all its variants; variants cannot exist on their own.
- **Product lifecycle workflow** — products move through a governed sequence of statuses: Draft, Enriching, Ready for Review, Published, Archived. The system enforces which transitions are allowed and prevents skipping steps.
- **Structured product attributes** — product-level attributes (like material, country of origin) are stored in a structured way that enables filtering, validation, and data integrity, replacing the current unstructured storage.

### What is included with modifications

**P1 — Should ship:**

- **Pricing as a proper value** — each variant carries a price and an optional comparison price as a structured value with amount and currency, rather than raw numbers. Simplified from multi-currency pricing tables used by larger platforms: only single-currency (RUB) is supported initially. No price lists or tiered pricing.
- **Concurrent edit protection** — when two catalog managers edit the same product simultaneously, the second save detects the conflict and warns the user instead of silently overwriting changes. Simplified from mandatory version tracking on every request: version is exposed in responses and accepted optionally on updates.
- **Variant uniqueness guarantee** — when adding a variant to a product, the system computes a fingerprint from the variant's attribute combination (e.g., "Red + Size L") and prevents duplicate combinations within the same product. Uses a standard hashing approach with built-in tools, no external dependencies.

### What is explicitly NOT included

**Deferred — will be built later:**

- **Product lifecycle events** (e.g., "product published", "price changed") — deferred until the core product management is complete and stable. Activate when downstream systems (search indexing, inventory, notifications) need to react to product changes.
- **Attribute completeness validation** — a service that checks whether all required attributes are filled before allowing a product to move to "Ready for Review." Deferred until the structured attribute storage is working. Activate when the review workflow needs automated quality gates.
- **Optimized product listings** — separate read-optimized views for product lists, detail pages, and storefront cards. Deferred until the product creation and editing workflows are complete. Activate before building any storefront-facing product listing or search.
- **Product media gallery** — managing product images and videos with roles (main image, gallery, size guide) and ordering. Deferred until core product management is stable. Activate before building the product gallery management feature.
- **Supplier as a managed entity** — full create/read/update operations for suppliers. Currently, products reference a supplier by ID only. Activate when the supplier portal or marketplace vendor onboarding becomes a business requirement.
- **Multi-category assignment** — allowing a product to appear in multiple categories beyond its primary category. Activate when the merchandising team requires cross-category product placement.

**Skipped — not planned:**

- **Multi-currency pricing** — per-variant pricing in multiple currencies. Not needed for a single-currency (RUB) marketplace.
- **Staged vs. live product editing** — editing a published product without affecting the live storefront version. Enterprise complexity with no current business need.
- **Barcode/GTIN fields on variants** — GS1 standard barcodes for retail and logistics integration. No current marketplace integration requirement.
- **Schema.org markup support** — structured data for search engine indexing. This is a frontend concern; the backend already exposes all required fields.

**Anti-patterns we explicitly avoid:**

- **Unstructured attribute storage** — storing product attributes as a single blob of unstructured data prevents filtering, validation, and data quality enforcement. We use structured, individually referenced attributes instead.
- **Variants as independent entities** — treating each variant as a standalone record breaks the ability to enforce rules across a product's variants (unique combinations, minimum active variants). Variants remain part of their parent product.
- **Unrestricted status changes** — allowing any status to change to any other (e.g., jumping from Archived directly to Published) bypasses quality gates. We enforce a governed state machine.
- **Floating-point prices** — storing prices as decimal numbers causes rounding errors in financial calculations. We store prices as whole numbers in the smallest currency unit (kopecks).
- **Loading everything for every query** — fetching all variants and media for simple product listings causes performance issues. We will use optimized read models (deferred to v1.1).

## 3. User scenarios

### Catalog manager (admin): Creating and publishing a product

**Happy path:**

1. The catalog manager opens the product management section and creates a new product by providing a title, URL-friendly slug, brand, primary category, and supplier.
2. The product is created in Draft status. The manager begins enriching it: adding a description, setting the country of origin, and filling in category-specific attributes (e.g., material, care instructions for a clothing category).
3. The manager transitions the product to Enriching status to signal that data entry is in progress.
4. The manager adds one or more variants (SKUs) to the product, each with a unique SKU code, a price, and variant-defining attributes (e.g., Color: Red, Size: L).
5. Once all required information is filled, the manager submits the product for review, moving it to Ready for Review.
6. After review, the manager publishes the product, making it visible on the storefront.

**Alternative scenarios:**

- The manager creates a simple product with only one variant (the default variant).
- The manager sends a product back from Ready for Review to Enriching because a reviewer found missing information.
- The manager archives a published product to temporarily hide it from the storefront, then later reactivates it by moving it back to Draft for re-editing.

**Edge cases:**

- What happens when two managers edit the same product at the same time? The second save receives a conflict notification and must reload the product before saving again (P1 feature).
- What happens when the manager tries to add a variant with the same attribute combination as an existing variant (e.g., two "Red, Size L")? The system rejects the duplicate (P1 feature).
- What happens when the manager tries to publish a product directly from Draft? The system blocks the transition — the product must go through Enriching and Ready for Review first.
- What happens when the manager deletes a product that has been referenced in past orders? The product is soft-deleted (hidden from listings but preserved in the database for order history integrity).

### Customer: Browsing and selecting product variants

**Happy path:**

1. The customer finds a product on the storefront (via search or category browsing).
2. The customer views the product detail page showing the title, description, product-level attributes (material, brand), and available variants.
3. The customer selects variant-defining attributes (e.g., chooses Color: Blue, Size: M) and sees the specific variant's price and availability.
4. The customer adds the selected variant to their cart.

**Alternative scenarios:**

- The customer views a simple product with only one variant — no variant selection is needed.
- The customer views the price range across all variants (e.g., "from 1,500 to 2,300 RUB") before selecting a specific variant.

**Edge cases:**

- What happens when a product has no active variants? The product should not appear on the storefront (enforced by requiring at least one active variant for Published status).

### Supplier: Tracking supplied products

**Happy path:**

1. The supplier's products are associated with their supplier ID during product creation by the catalog manager.
2. The supplier can be identified on any product record through the supplier reference.

**Alternative scenarios:**

- A product is created without a supplier reference (supplier is optional).

**Edge cases:**

- What happens when a supplier is no longer active? Products remain associated with the supplier ID for historical reference; the supplier reference is not deleted.

## 4. Functional requirements

### P0 — Must have

#### FR-001: Product and variant management (aggregate root with CRUD)

- **What:** The system provides full create, read, update, and soft-delete operations for products and their variants (SKUs). A product is the central managed unit that owns all its variants. Variants cannot be created, moved, or managed outside the context of their parent product.
- **Why:** This is the foundational capability — every other feature depends on having a product entity with proper business rules. Every major e-commerce platform (Shopify, Medusa, Saleor, Spree, commercetools) treats the product as the primary aggregate that owns its variants. Without this, there is no catalog to manage. Answers: "Is Product the single aggregate root containing SKUs as child entities, or is SKU a separate aggregate?" — Product is the aggregate root; SKU is a child entity.
- **Acceptance criteria:**
  - When a catalog manager sends a create-product request with title, slug, brand ID, and primary category ID, the system creates a product in Draft status and returns the product ID.
  - When a catalog manager sends a request to add a variant to product P with SKU code "SKU-001", price 150000 (kopecks), and currency "RUB", the system creates the variant under product P and returns the variant ID.
  - When a catalog manager sends an update request for product P changing the title to "New Title", the system updates the product and the title is reflected in subsequent reads.
  - When a catalog manager sends a delete request for product P, the product is soft-deleted (hidden from listings but preserved in the database). Subsequent list queries do not return this product. A direct get-by-ID still returns it with a deletion marker.
  - When a catalog manager sends a request to delete variant V from product P, the variant is soft-deleted. The product itself remains active.
  - When a request attempts to create a variant without specifying a parent product, the system rejects it with a validation error.
- **Industry reference:** Shopify, Medusa, Saleor, Spree, commercetools, DDD aggregate design guidance

#### FR-002: Product status lifecycle with governed transitions

- **What:** Every product has a status that follows a defined workflow: Draft -> Enriching -> Ready for Review -> Published -> Archived. The system enforces which transitions are allowed, preventing invalid jumps (e.g., Draft directly to Published). Certain backward transitions are permitted (Enriching back to Draft, Ready for Review back to Enriching, Archived back to Draft). Published products cannot go directly back to Draft or Enriching — they must be Archived first.
- **Why:** Without governed status transitions, incomplete or unreviewed products could appear on the storefront, damaging data quality and customer trust. All enterprise catalog platforms (Shopify, Akeneo, Saleor, commercetools) enforce lifecycle rules. The existing database already defines these five statuses, but no business logic enforces the transition rules. Answers: "What status transitions are allowed in the product lifecycle, and can you go backward?" — yes, with restrictions: Published must go through Archived before returning to Draft.
- **Acceptance criteria:**
  - When a product is in Draft status and a transition to Enriching is requested, the system allows it and updates the status to Enriching.
  - When a product is in Draft status and a transition to Published is requested, the system rejects it with an error listing the allowed transitions (only Enriching is allowed from Draft).
  - When a product is in Published status and a transition to Draft is requested, the system rejects it with an error indicating the product must be Archived first.
  - When a product is in Published status and a transition to Archived is requested, the system allows it.
  - When a product is in Archived status and a transition to Draft is requested, the system allows it (reactivation path).
  - When a product is in Ready for Review status and a transition to Enriching is requested, the system allows it (send-back-for-fixes path).
  - The complete set of allowed transitions is: Draft->Enriching, Enriching->Draft, Enriching->Ready for Review, Ready for Review->Enriching, Ready for Review->Published, Published->Archived, Archived->Draft.
- **Industry reference:** Shopify, Medusa, Saleor, WooCommerce, Akeneo, commercetools

#### FR-003: Structured product attribute values (EAV pivot)

- **What:** Product-level attributes (e.g., material, care instructions) are stored as individually referenced records linking the product to a specific attribute and its value, rather than as unstructured data. Each record connects a product to an attribute definition and an attribute value from the existing attribute dictionary. This enables filtering products by attribute values, enforcing that attribute values reference valid dictionary entries, and validating completeness against category-specific attribute requirements.
- **Why:** The current unstructured storage prevents filtering by attribute values in SQL queries, cannot enforce that values reference valid dictionary entries, and makes completeness validation impossible. Industry leaders (Saleor, Magento, commercetools, Akeneo) all use structured attribute storage. The existing variant-level attribute storage already follows this pattern successfully — product-level attributes should match. Answers: "How should product EAV attributes be validated on save — through CategoryAttributeBinding or a separate validation service?" — structured storage is the prerequisite; validation service is deferred.
- **Acceptance criteria:**
  - When a catalog manager assigns attribute "Material" with value "Cotton" to product P, the system creates a structured record linking product P to attribute "Material" and value "Cotton". Subsequent queries for product P's attributes return this assignment.
  - When a catalog manager attempts to assign the same attribute twice to the same product (e.g., two different values for "Material"), the system rejects the second assignment with a duplicate-attribute error.
  - When a catalog manager removes attribute "Material" from product P, the assignment record is deleted. The attribute definition and value remain in the dictionary unaffected.
  - When a catalog manager queries product P's attributes, the system returns a list of all assigned attributes with their values, grouped by attribute.
  - When a query filters products by attribute value (e.g., "all products where Material = Cotton"), the system returns matching products efficiently using indexed lookups.
  - When a product is soft-deleted, its attribute assignments are preserved in the database (not cascade-deleted) for historical integrity.
- **Industry reference:** Saleor, Magento, commercetools, Akeneo

### P1 — Should have

#### FR-004: Money value object for variant pricing

- **What:** Each variant (SKU) carries its price and optional comparison price (the "was" price for sale displays) as a structured value combining amount and currency, rather than as separate raw number fields. Prices are stored as whole numbers in the smallest currency unit (kopecks for RUB). The product exposes a computed minimum and maximum price across all its active variants for display purposes.
- **Why:** A structured price value prevents accidental misuse of raw numbers (e.g., mixing currencies, treating kopecks as rubles). Every major platform (Shopify, Medusa, Saleor, Spree, commercetools, Stripe) recommends this pattern. The existing database already stores prices as integers correctly — this adds type safety in the business logic layer.
- **Simplification:** Multi-currency pricing tables (used by Medusa and commercetools) are not needed. Only single-currency (RUB) is supported. No price lists or tiered pricing. The comparison price is optional.
- **Acceptance criteria:**
  - When a variant is created with price 150000 and currency "RUB", the system stores and returns the price as a structured value: amount=150000, currency="RUB".
  - When a variant has both a price (200000) and a comparison price (250000), the system validates that the comparison price is greater than the regular price. If comparison price is less than or equal to regular price, the system rejects with a validation error.
  - When a product has three active variants with prices 150000, 200000, and 250000, querying the product returns min_price=150000 and max_price=250000.
  - When a variant's price is updated from 150000 to 180000, the product's computed min/max price range updates accordingly.
- **Industry reference:** Shopify, Medusa, Saleor, Spree, commercetools, Stripe

#### FR-005: Optimistic locking for concurrent edit protection

- **What:** Products and variants carry a version number that increments on every change. When reading a product, the version is included in the response. When updating, the caller can include the expected version number. If the product has been modified since it was read (version mismatch), the update is rejected with a conflict error.
- **Why:** Multiple catalog managers may edit the same product simultaneously. Without conflict detection, the last save silently overwrites all previous changes. commercetools mandates version on every update; this implementation makes it optional but available.
- **Simplification:** Version is exposed in responses and accepted optionally in update requests (not mandatory as in commercetools). The database already enforces version tracking — this surfaces it through the API.
- **Acceptance criteria:**
  - When a product is read, the response includes a `version` field with the current version number (e.g., version=3).
  - When an update request includes version=3 and the product's current version is 3, the update succeeds and the version increments to 4.
  - When an update request includes version=2 but the product's current version is 3, the system rejects the update with a 409 Conflict error indicating a concurrent modification.
  - When an update request omits the version field, the update proceeds without conflict checking (backward-compatible behavior).
- **Industry reference:** commercetools, DDD aggregate design guidance, Magento

#### FR-006: Variant hash for SKU uniqueness within a product

- **What:** When a variant is added to a product, the system computes a deterministic fingerprint from the variant's attribute combination (e.g., Color=Red + Size=L). If another variant on the same product already has the same fingerprint, the new variant is rejected. This prevents duplicate variant combinations within a product.
- **Why:** Without this check, a product could end up with two identical "Red, Size L" variants, causing confusion for customers and inventory tracking issues. The existing database has a column reserved for this fingerprint but no logic to compute or enforce it.
- **Simplification:** Uses standard library hashing (no external dependencies). The fingerprint includes the product ID for global uniqueness. Uniqueness is checked within the product boundary before saving.
- **Acceptance criteria:**
  - When variant V1 is added to product P with attributes {Color: Red, Size: L}, and variant V2 is then added to product P with the same attributes {Color: Red, Size: L}, the system rejects V2 with a "duplicate variant combination" error.
  - When variant V1 has attributes {Color: Red, Size: L} on product P1, and variant V2 has the same attributes {Color: Red, Size: L} on a different product P2, the system allows V2 (uniqueness is per-product, not global).
  - When variant V1 has attributes {Color: Red, Size: L} and variant V2 has attributes {Color: Blue, Size: L} on the same product, the system allows both (different attribute combinations produce different fingerprints).
  - When a variant's attributes are updated to match an existing sibling variant's attributes, the system rejects the update with a "duplicate variant combination" error.
- **Industry reference:** commercetools (CombinationUnique constraint), DDD aggregate invariant enforcement

## 5. Data model

### Entities

#### `Product`

- **Purpose:** The central catalog entity representing a sellable item. Owns all its variants, attributes, and (in the future) media assets.
- **Key attributes:**
  - `id` — UUID — unique identifier
  - `slug` — string — URL-friendly unique identifier for SEO routing
  - `title` — localized text (i18n) — display name
  - `description` — localized text (i18n) — full product description
  - `status` — enum (DRAFT, ENRICHING, READY_FOR_REVIEW, PUBLISHED, ARCHIVED) — current lifecycle status
  - `brand_id` — UUID (FK) — reference to the brand (existing entity)
  - `primary_category_id` — UUID (FK) — reference to the primary category (existing entity); determines which attributes apply
  - `supplier_id` — UUID (FK, nullable) — reference to the supplier
  - `country_of_origin` — string(2) — ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 country code
  - `tags` — list of strings — free-form tags for internal organization
  - `version` — integer — optimistic locking version counter (P1)
  - `deleted_at` — timestamp (nullable) — soft-delete marker
  - `created_at` — timestamp — creation time
  - `updated_at` — timestamp — last modification time
- **Relationships:**
  - Has many `SKU` (one-to-many, owned child entities)
  - Has many `ProductAttributeValue` (one-to-many, product-level EAV)
  - Belongs to `Brand` (many-to-one)
  - Belongs to `Category` (many-to-one, via primary_category_id)
- **Design rationale:** Product is the aggregate root per universal industry consensus (Shopify, Medusa, Saleor, Spree, commercetools). All modifications to variants and attributes go through the Product boundary to enforce invariants.

#### `SKU`

- **Purpose:** A purchasable variant of a product, defined by a unique combination of variant-level attributes (e.g., Color + Size).
- **Key attributes:**
  - `id` — UUID — unique identifier
  - `product_id` — UUID (FK) — reference to the parent product
  - `sku_code` — string — human-readable stock-keeping unit code (unique among non-deleted SKUs)
  - `variant_hash` — string(64) — deterministic fingerprint of the variant's attribute combination; enforces uniqueness within the product (P1)
  - `price` — Money (amount: integer + currency: string) — the selling price in smallest currency unit (P1: Money VO; MVP: raw integer)
  - `compare_at_price` — Money (nullable) — the original/comparison price for sale displays
  - `currency` — string(3) — ISO 4217 currency code (default: RUB)
  - `is_active` — boolean — whether this variant is available for purchase
  - `version` — integer — optimistic locking version counter (P1)
  - `deleted_at` — timestamp (nullable) — soft-delete marker
- **Relationships:**
  - Belongs to `Product` (many-to-one, child entity within aggregate)
  - Has many `SKUAttributeValueLink` (existing variant-level EAV)
- **Design rationale:** SKU is a child entity within the Product aggregate, not an independent entity. The product enforces variant uniqueness via `variant_hash` and requires at least one active SKU for Published status.

#### `ProductAttributeValue`

- **Purpose:** Links a product to an attribute and its value from the attribute dictionary. Replaces the unstructured JSONB storage with individually referenced, queryable records.
- **Key attributes:**
  - `id` — UUID — unique identifier
  - `product_id` — UUID (FK) — reference to the product
  - `attribute_id` — UUID (FK) — reference to the attribute definition (existing entity)
  - `attribute_value_id` — UUID (FK) — reference to the attribute value (existing entity)
- **Relationships:**
  - Belongs to `Product` (many-to-one)
  - References `Attribute` (many-to-one, existing entity)
  - References `AttributeValue` (many-to-one, existing entity)
- **Constraints:**
  - Unique on (`product_id`, `attribute_id`) — one value per attribute per product
- **Design rationale:** Mirrors the existing `SKUAttributeValueLink` pattern for variant-level attributes. Enables SQL-based filtering, foreign key integrity, and completeness validation against `CategoryAttributeBinding` requirements.

#### `Money` (value object, P1)

- **Purpose:** Represents a monetary amount with its currency, ensuring type safety and preventing raw-number misuse in pricing logic.
- **Key attributes:**
  - `amount` — integer — price in smallest currency unit (kopecks for RUB)
  - `currency` — string(3) — ISO 4217 currency code
- **Design rationale:** Follows Stripe and industry best practice of storing money as integers. Wraps existing database fields (no migration needed) to add type safety in business logic. Simplified from multi-currency MoneyAmount tables — single currency only.

### Entity relationship summary

A `Product` has many `SKU` variants (child entities within the aggregate). Each `SKU` is linked to specific variant-level attribute values through the existing `SKUAttributeValueLink` table. A `Product` also has many `ProductAttributeValue` records for product-level attributes, each referencing an `Attribute` definition and an `AttributeValue` from the dictionary. The `Product` belongs to one `Brand` and one primary `Category`. The primary category determines which attributes are relevant for the product through existing `CategoryAttributeBinding` records.

### Migration notes

The database tables for `Product` (products) and `SKU` (skus) already exist with all required columns. A new `product_attribute_values` table must be created for the `ProductAttributeValue` entity. The existing `Product.attributes` JSONB column will be retained temporarily during transition — existing JSONB data (if any) must be audited and migrated to the new structured table before the JSONB column is dropped. No changes to existing tables for `Brand`, `Category`, `Attribute`, `AttributeValue`, `CategoryAttributeBinding`, or `SKUAttributeValueLink`.

### Request/response patterns

**Pagination:** List endpoints support cursor-based pagination with `limit` and `after` parameters, consistent with existing catalog endpoints.

**Filtering:** Product list endpoints (deferred to v1.1 read models) will support filtering by status, brand, category, and attribute values. Individual product GET returns the full aggregate.

**Error responses:** Standard error format with `error_code`, `message`, and `details` fields. Key error codes:

- `PRODUCT_NOT_FOUND` — 404
- `INVALID_STATUS_TRANSITION` — 422, includes allowed transitions in details
- `DUPLICATE_ATTRIBUTE` — 409, when assigning an attribute already present on the product
- `DUPLICATE_VARIANT_COMBINATION` — 409, when variant hash matches an existing sibling (P1)
- `CONCURRENT_MODIFICATION` — 409, when version mismatch detected (P1)
- `VALIDATION_ERROR` — 422, for missing required fields or invalid data

**Versioning:** Product and SKU responses include a `version` field (P1). Update requests accept an optional `version` field for conflict detection.

### Admin interface

The API supports the following admin workflows:

- **Product list view** — browse products filtered by status, brand, or category (requires deferred read models for optimal performance; MVP can use aggregate loading)
- **Product detail/edit view** — view and modify all product fields, manage variants and attributes
- **Status transition actions** — dedicated action to change product status with validation feedback on disallowed transitions
- **Variant management** — add, edit, and remove variants within the product detail view

## 7. Non-functional requirements

### Performance

- API response time for single product read: p95 < 200ms
- API response time for product create/update: p95 < 300ms
- Product attribute assignment: p95 < 150ms
- Variant addition with hash computation: p95 < 200ms

### Security

- **Authentication:** All product management endpoints require a valid authenticated session.
- **Authorization:** Only users with the catalog manager (admin) role can create, update, delete, or transition products. Customer-facing read endpoints (deferred to v1.1) will be publicly accessible for published products only.
- **Input validation:** All request payloads validated at the API boundary — title length limits, slug format (lowercase alphanumeric with hyphens), valid UUID references for brand/category/supplier, ISO currency and country codes.
- **Audit:** All product mutations (create, update, delete, status change, variant add/remove) are logged with the acting user ID and timestamp via structured logging.

### Scalability

- **Volume:** The system should handle up to 100,000 products with an average of 5 variants each (500,000 SKU records) and up to 50 product-level attributes per product.
- **Concurrency:** Up to 20 concurrent catalog managers editing different products simultaneously. Optimistic locking (P1) handles the case of concurrent edits to the same product.

### Compliance

- **ISO 4217** — Currency fields use 3-letter ISO 4217 codes. Already implemented in the database (SKU.currency = String(3), default "RUB").
- **ISO 3166-1 alpha-2** — Country of origin uses 2-letter country codes. Already implemented in the database (Product.country_of_origin = String(2)).
- **GS1 GTIN/EAN/UPC** — Not applicable for MVP. Relevant when products are sold through physical retail or external marketplaces.

## 10. Roadmap

### MVP (P0)

Implementation order respects dependencies:

1. **Product aggregate root with CRUD** — create the Product and SKU domain entities, repository interface, command handlers, presentation schemas, and API endpoints. No dependencies.
2. **Product status lifecycle FSM** — add governed status transition methods to the Product entity. Depends on #1 (FSM methods live on the Product entity).
3. **Structured product attribute values** — create the ProductAttributeValue entity, ORM model, migration, and attribute assignment endpoints. Can proceed in parallel with #2 after #1 is complete.

**Definition of done:**

- All 12 MVP API endpoints return correct responses for happy-path and error scenarios
- Status transitions enforce the governed FSM (7 allowed transitions, all others rejected)
- Product attribute values are stored as structured records with foreign key integrity
- Soft-delete works for both products and variants
- All unit tests pass; architecture boundary tests pass
- API documented with request/response examples

### v1.1 (P1)

- **Money value object** — introduce Money VO for price and compare_at_price on SKU. Depends on Product aggregate root.
- **Optimistic locking in domain** — add version field to Product and SKU entities; surface in API. Depends on Product aggregate root.
- **Variant hash algorithm** — compute SHA-256 fingerprint in Product.add_sku(); enforce uniqueness. Depends on Product aggregate root.

### Future

- **Domain events for product lifecycle** — activate after Product aggregate and FSM are complete; required before search indexing and inventory consumers can react to product changes.
- **EAV completeness validation** — activate after ProductAttributeValue pivot is working; required before the Enriching-to-Ready for Review guard can be enforced automatically.
- **Optimized product read models** — activate after Product CRUD command handlers are complete and tested; required before any storefront-facing product listing or search endpoint.
- **Product media gallery** — activate after Product CRUD is stable; required before the product gallery management feature.
- **Supplier domain entity** — activate when supplier portal or marketplace vendor onboarding becomes a business requirement.
- **Multi-category assignment** — activate when the merchandising team requires cross-category product placement beyond the primary category.
